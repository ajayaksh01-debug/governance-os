# EU AI Act

## What It Is

The EU AI Act (Regulation (EU) 2024/1689) is the world's first comprehensive legal framework for artificial intelligence. Published in the Official Journal of the European Union on 12 July 2024, it entered into force on 1 August 2024. It applies to providers, deployers, importers, distributors, and users of AI systems — wherever they are based — if the AI system's output is used in the EU.

The Act takes a risk-based approach: obligations are proportionate to the risk an AI system poses to fundamental rights, safety, and societal values.

---

## Why It Matters

The EU AI Act is the regulatory event horizon for global AI governance. It applies extraterritorially — a US, Indian, or UK company providing an AI system used in the EU must comply. For enterprises with EU operations, EU customers, or EU-facing AI products, compliance is not optional.

The Act also sets the global template. Regulators in the UK, India, Canada, and elsewhere are watching closely, and market precedent means that EU AI Act compliance often becomes the baseline for global enterprise AI governance programmes.

## Risk Tiers

The Act classifies AI systems into four risk tiers, with obligations escalating at each level.

### Unacceptable Risk — Prohibited
Certain AI uses are banned outright. Prohibitions include:

- Biometric categorisation systems that infer protected characteristics (race, religion, political opinion, sexual orientation) from biometric data
- Social scoring by public or private entities that leads to detrimental treatment
- AI that exploits vulnerabilities of specific groups (age, disability, social situation)
- Real-time remote biometric identification in public spaces by law enforcement (with narrow exceptions)
- Emotion recognition in workplaces and educational institutions
- Manipulation of human behaviour through subliminal techniques

Prohibitions apply from 2 February 2025.

### High Risk
High-risk AI systems face the most significant compliance burden. Two categories:

**Annex I — Safety-critical products:** AI embedded in regulated products (medical devices, vehicles, machinery, toys) where the AI is the safety component.

**Annex III — Standalone high-risk AI:** Eight domains explicitly listed:
1. Biometric identification and categorisation
2. Critical infrastructure management (water, gas, electricity, transport)
3. Education and vocational training (access, evaluation, assessment)
4. Employment and worker management (recruitment, CV screening, task allocation, performance monitoring)
5. Access to essential private and public services (credit scoring, insurance underwriting)
6. Law enforcement (risk assessments, polygraphs, profiling)
7. Migration, asylum, and border control
8. Administration of justice and democratic processes

**Key obligations for high-risk AI:**
- Conformity assessment before deployment
- Registration in the EU AI Act database (for most systems)
- Risk management system maintained throughout lifecycle
- Data governance requirements for training, validation, and test data
- Technical documentation and record-keeping
- Transparency and provision of information to deployers
- Human oversight measures designed into the system
- Accuracy, robustness, and cybersecurity requirements
- Post-market monitoring

### Limited Risk
AI systems that interact with humans (chatbots, deepfakes, AI-generated content) must disclose that the user is interacting with AI. Content generated by AI must be marked as such where technically feasible.

### Minimal Risk
All other AI systems. No mandatory requirements, though providers are encouraged to apply voluntary codes of conduct.

## General Purpose AI (GPAI) Models

The Act introduces a separate regime for foundation models and general purpose AI. All GPAI model providers must:

- Maintain technical documentation
- Comply with EU copyright law
- Publish a summary of training data

Providers of GPAI models with "systemic risk" (currently defined as training compute above 10²⁵ FLOPs, or those designated by the Commission) face additional obligations:
- Model evaluation including adversarial testing
- Incident reporting to the Commission
- Cybersecurity protections
- Energy efficiency reporting

This regime directly affects providers of models like GPT-4, Claude, Gemini, and Llama — and any organisation fine-tuning these at scale.

## Compliance Timeline

| Date | Obligation |
|---|---|
| 1 August 2024 | Act enters into force |
| 2 February 2025 | Prohibitions on unacceptable risk AI apply |
| 2 August 2025 | GPAI model obligations apply; governance obligations apply |
| 2 August 2026 | High-risk AI (Annex III) obligations fully apply |
| 2 August 2027 | High-risk AI (Annex I, safety products) obligations fully apply |

## Enforcement and Penalties

The Act is enforced by national market surveillance authorities, with oversight coordination by the newly established European AI Office.

Penalties:
- Prohibited AI uses: up to €35 million or 7% of global annual turnover (whichever is higher)
- Other violations: up to €15 million or 3% of global annual turnover
- Incorrect or misleading information to authorities: up to €7.5 million or 1.5% of global annual turnover

SME and startup provisions apply — penalties are proportionate, but the obligations themselves are not reduced.

## BFSI Implications

Financial services AI deployments face specific obligations under the Act:

- **Credit scoring and insurance underwriting** are explicitly listed as high-risk (Annex III). Full high-risk obligations apply.
- **Fraud detection** systems used for real-time decisions on individuals are likely high-risk depending on design and impact.
- **Algorithmic trading** and risk modelling systems require careful classification — Annex III coverage depends on whether they make decisions with significant effects on individuals.
- **Customer-facing AI** (chatbots, robo-advisors) face transparency obligations at minimum, and high-risk obligations if they materially affect access to financial services.

Financial services firms already subject to EBA, ECB, and ESMA AI guidance should map those obligations against the Act — overlap is significant and a unified compliance approach is more efficient.

## Relationship to Other Frameworks

| Framework | Relationship |
|---|---|
| ISO 42001 | ISO 42001 is expected to become the harmonised standard for EU AI Act conformity assessment. Certification against ISO 42001 will likely satisfy many high-risk documentation and governance requirements. |
| NIST AI RMF | Philosophically aligned — both use risk-based approaches. Not directly recognised by the Act, but NIST alignment supports the governance substance the Act requires. |
| GDPR | AI Act and GDPR overlap significantly for AI systems processing personal data. DPIA requirements under GDPR align with AI Act impact assessment obligations. Joint compliance programmes are the most efficient approach. |

## Recommendations

1. Conduct an AI inventory audit immediately — organisations cannot assess EU AI Act exposure without knowing what AI systems they have, what they do, and whether they affect EU individuals.
2. Classify each AI system against the risk tiers — prohibited, high-risk (Annex I or III), limited risk, or minimal risk. This classification drives the compliance roadmap.
3. For high-risk AI systems, begin conformity assessment preparation now — the 2026 deadline for Annex III systems is closer than it appears given the documentation and testing effort required.
4. Treat credit scoring, insurance, and recruitment AI as high-risk by default — these are explicitly listed in Annex III with no ambiguity.
5. Establish GPAI model governance for any organisation using third-party foundation models — supplier obligations flow down to deployers in some contexts, and due diligence on model providers is now a legal requirement.
