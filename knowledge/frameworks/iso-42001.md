# ISO 42001 — AI Management System Standard

## What It Is

ISO/IEC 42001:2023 is the world's first international standard for AI management systems. Published in December 2023, it provides a framework for organisations to establish, implement, maintain, and continually improve the responsible development and use of AI.

It follows the same high-level structure (Annex SL) as ISO 27001 and ISO 9001, making integration with existing management systems straightforward for organisations already operating within those frameworks.

---

## Why It Matters

ISO 42001 is becoming the de facto baseline for demonstrable AI governance. Regulators, enterprise procurement teams, and insurers are beginning to reference it as evidence of governance maturity. In regulated sectors — financial services, healthcare, critical infrastructure — it is increasingly a prerequisite for deploying AI in high-stakes use cases.

The standard is also the primary mechanism through which organisations can demonstrate alignment with the EU AI Act's general governance requirements, as the Act explicitly anticipates harmonised standards as a conformity pathway.

---

## Scope and Applicability

ISO 42001 applies to any organisation that:
- Develops AI systems (as a provider)
- Deploys AI systems (as a deployer)
- Uses AI-enabled products or services in its operations

It is sector-agnostic and scale-neutral. Requirements are proportionate — a small organisation deploying a single AI model faces a different compliance burden than a large provider developing foundation models.

---

## Key Requirements

### Clause 4 — Context of the Organisation
The organisation must understand its internal and external context as it relates to AI, identify interested parties (regulators, customers, affected individuals), and define the scope of the AI Management System (AIMS).

### Clause 5 — Leadership
Top management must demonstrate visible commitment to the AIMS. This includes establishing an AI policy, assigning roles and responsibilities, and ensuring AI governance is integrated into strategic planning — not siloed in a technology or legal team.

### Clause 6 — Planning
Organisations must conduct AI risk assessments that address risks arising from AI systems themselves (not just to the organisation, but to affected individuals and society). An AI impact assessment methodology must be defined.

### Clause 7 — Support
Resource requirements for AI governance — people, infrastructure, competence, and awareness — must be formally addressed. Documentation must be controlled and maintained.

### Clause 8 — Operation
The operational core of the standard. Organisations must:
- Manage the AI system lifecycle (development, testing, deployment, retirement)
- Implement controls to address identified risks
- Manage third-party AI providers and the supply chain
- Conduct AI impact assessments for new or significantly changed AI systems

### Clause 9 — Performance Evaluation
Internal audits, management reviews, and performance monitoring against defined objectives. AI systems must be monitored for performance degradation, bias drift, and unexpected behaviour in production.

### Clause 10 — Improvement
Nonconformities must be addressed with root cause analysis and corrective action. The AIMS must be continuously improved based on audit findings and performance data.

## Annex A — Controls Reference

Annex A provides 38 controls across 9 control categories. Key controls include:

| Control Area | Examples |
|---|---|
| AI policy and governance | AI policy, roles and responsibilities, AI risk criteria |
| AI risk assessment | Impact assessment, data quality assessment, bias evaluation |
| AI system lifecycle | Design requirements, testing, validation, monitoring |
| Data governance | Data provenance, data minimisation, third-party data |
| Supply chain | Third-party AI provider assessment, contractual controls |
| Human oversight | Human review requirements, override mechanisms |
| Incident management | AI incident response, post-incident review |
| Transparency | Disclosure requirements, explainability documentation |
| Monitoring | Production monitoring, performance metrics, drift detection |

## Certification Pathway

Certification follows the same model as ISO 27001:
1. **Gap assessment** — measure current state against requirements
2. **Remediation** — close identified gaps through policy, process, and technical controls
3. **Stage 1 audit** — documentation review by accredited certification body
4. **Stage 2 audit** — on-site evidence review and interviews
5. **Certification** — issued for three years with annual surveillance audits

Certification requires engagement with an accredited certification body. Self-declaration against the standard is also an option for organisations not yet seeking third-party certification.

---

## Business Impact

**For providers:** Certification signals trustworthy AI development to enterprise customers and regulators. It reduces friction in procurement due diligence and provides a defensible governance posture.

**For deployers:** The standard provides a structured framework for managing AI risk across a portfolio of use cases — replacing ad hoc assessments with a systematic approach.

**For regulated entities:** ISO 42001 is increasingly referenced alongside sector-specific obligations (SR 11-7 in banking, ICO guidance in the UK). Alignment reduces duplicative compliance effort.

## Relationship to Other Frameworks

| Framework | Relationship |
|---|---|
| NIST AI RMF | Complementary. NIST provides a risk management vocabulary; ISO 42001 provides a certifiable management system structure. |
| EU AI Act | ISO 42001 is expected to become a harmonised standard supporting EU AI Act conformity assessment for high-risk AI systems. |
| ISO 27001 | Directly integrable. Organisations with ISO 27001 can extend their ISMS to an AIMS using shared infrastructure. |
| OWASP LLM Top 10 | OWASP provides the technical threat taxonomy; ISO 42001 provides the management system to govern against those threats. |

---

## Recommendations

1. Use ISO 42001 as the primary governance framework for enterprise AI programmes — it is the only certifiable international standard and integrates with existing management systems.
2. Begin with a gap assessment scoped to the organisation's highest-risk AI use cases, not the full AI portfolio.
3. Assign the AIMS ownership to a role with cross-functional authority (CISO, CRO, or a dedicated Chief AI Officer) — not purely to a technology team.
4. For BFSI clients, align ISO 42001 controls explicitly with SR 11-7 (model risk management) and applicable central bank guidance to avoid duplicative effort.
5. Treat certification as a business development asset, not only a compliance exercise — it signals governance maturity to enterprise buyers and regulators.
